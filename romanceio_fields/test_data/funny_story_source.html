Funny Story

Emily Henry

Rated: 4.32 of 5 stars
4.32 · Steam/Spice level: 3 of 5
Open door [?] · 805 ratings · 416 pages · Published: 23 Apr 2024
A shimmering, joyful new novel about a pair of opposites with the wrong thing in common, from #1 New York Times bestselling author Emily Henry.

Daphne always loved the way her fiancé Peter told their story. How they met (on a blustery day), fell in love (over an errant hat), and moved back to his lakeside hometown to begin their life together. He really was good at telling it…right up until the moment he realized he was actually in love with his childhood best friend Petra.
 
Which is how Daphne begins her new Stranded in beautiful Waning Bay, Michigan, without friends or family but with a dream job as a children’s librarian (that barely pays the bills), and proposing to be roommates with the only person who could possibly understand her Petra’s ex, Miles Nowak.
 
 Scruffy and chaotic—with a penchant for taking solace in the sounds of heart break love ballads —Miles is exactly the opposite of practical, buttoned up Daphne, whose coworkers know so little about her they have a running bet that she’s either FBI or in witness protection. The roommates mainly avoid one another, until one day, while drowning their sorrows, they form a tenuous friendship and a plan. If said plan also involves posting deliberately misleading photos of their summer adventures together, well, who could blame them?
 
But it’s all just for show, of course, because there’s no way Daphne would actually start her new chapter by falling in love with her ex-fiancé’s new fiancée’s ex…right? Rated '3/5 - Open door' for steam/spice/heat level.

    • snooblette rated it
      8 months ago
      After the Happy-Place-shaped blip, SHE'S BACK. The four-month-old friendship in this book blows the Happy Place 8-year-friendship out of the water. 

      The good:
      - Daphne and Miles were so believable. They felt real. Especially their progression from strangers to friends to lovers should be included in a Masterclass in romance writing. Emily Henry didn't take the fake dating trope top far. Instead, she focused on actually building a friendship between the two characters which only added to the tension. 
      - You know the romance is good when I am happy and giggly even though I am not at all attracted to the MMC. Weed-infused, Crocs-wearing golden retriever? Pass. But Miles is perfect for Daphne. 
      - Lots of life lessons and self growth in this book. This book tackles a lot. There are daddy issues, commitment issues, abandonment issues. At the start of the book, I thought I was going to get bogged down by all of the drama (Kind of like how Just For the Summer by Abby Jimenez made me feel). But the story actually felt light. The characters were a lot smarter, and came to their senses a lot sooner than expected. They weren't dramatic for the sake of angst or the plot. They behave like proper 30 year olds. It was refreshing. 
      - The friendship between Daphne and Ashleigh was mature and delightful. It actually felt like two 30-year olds forming a brand new friendship. I loved seeing them grow alongside each other. 

      The less good:
      - Julia's inclusion felt out of place. She already read the most like a cartoon character. I am not sure she was really needed. 
      - There was 11-times too many instances of the word "chortle". Probably one of the least attractive words ever. To read about Daphne and Miles chortling was.... Not it.

    • Dellydoodles rated it
      1 month ago
      As I sit here on a beach during the summer holidays, I reflect on why I love romance books so much. They offer a great time and I can easily read them in a day. They’re light, they’re fun, there’s little thought involved. But you best believe that I’ll be here to critically review every single one of them.

      My first Emily Henry, my cherry’s been popped. And hey, I’m a fan.

      In Funny Story, Daphne our MC is going through one of the roughest break ups. Her ex-fiancé, Peter, has ditched her for his childhood best friend, Petra, a couple of days before their wedding. To make things even messier, Daphne ends up sharing a house with Miles, Petra’s ex, who’s equally jaded and just as baffled by their respective betrayals. Their shared misery and tequila-fueled bonding lead to a fake-dating scheme, because, why not? Everything ends in fake dating these days. But anywho we’re having fun.

      Now for the romance. Miles, the love interest, is… fine. He’s kind, a bit of a stoner, but definitely has some charm. But he’s also a bit too eh for my taste. While the progression of their relationship is sweet and believable, it’s missing the passion and intensity that make a romance JUICY. The fake-dating setup adds some tension in the early chapters, but it fizzles out before it becomes run of the mill just normal dating stuff. This book could have been a little sexier is all I’m saying.

      The real highlight, though, is how the book focuses on relationships beyond the romance. Daphne’s budding friendship with Ashleigh, for example, is heartwarming and feels incredibly real. It’s rare to see a romance novel give so much weight to platonic connections, but Henry pulls it off beautifully. There’s also a strong message here about self-discovery: learning to be your own person in a relationship rather than just absorbing your partner’s personality. This theme is woven throughout the story without feeling preachy, and it adds a lot of depth to Daphne’s journey.

      I like Henry’s writing. It felt really realistic and grounded in comparison to other romance books I’ve read. And genuinely was kind of funny. As in funny haha. As in I actually did laugh out loud once or twice. A rare sight for me indeed. 3/5 stars.

    • illiteratewench rated it
      2 months ago
      I'm so happy Emily Henry has done it again for me, giving me my third 5 star read out of the four of her books I've read (I'm avoiding Happy Place because I'm weary of the second chance aspect).

      Firstly, I love that Miles is a bit different than other Emily Henry men, who are usually bookish and nerdy in some way, and can tend to be the quiet responsible ones. Miles is still an Emily Henry man at heart, he's caring and thoughtful and mature, but he's outwardly a bit different, having nautical tattoos and smoking weed and sleeping in late to work nights (so scandalous I know). I loved that the fake dating aspect wasn't the main thing of this book. It had some extremely funny moments and I'm glad it didn't overwhelm the book with trying to adhere to a troupe, especially as TikTok and other social media has heavily "troupified" books and book marketing.

      Like with all her books, Miles is an amazing love interest, and while he has his own things going on (I love the representation of going no contact with abusive parents), this is definitely Daphne's story as she grapples with the end of her engagement with Peter, and takes a look at her attachment and relationship issues. Since Daphne relocated to Peter's hometown, her social life has been intwined with his, so when their engagement ends, she finds herself suddenly very alone, and struggling to make new friends in her 30's. I love that this is Daphne's time to reflect on herself. I will say that in the final third of the book, I did wish there was more romance, but I understood that the plot point <spoiler> with her dad </spoiler> was very important for her to unpack her childhood and the way she navigates relationships.

      Loved this book, it had me laughing as I read it in public (at the bus stop, on the bus, as they called my name out at the pharmacy...). If you are a fan of Emily Henry, I think you will like it as well.
