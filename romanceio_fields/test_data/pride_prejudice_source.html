Pride and Prejudice

Jane Austen

Rated: 4.54 of 5 stars
4.54 · Steam/Spice level: 1 of 5
Glimpses and kisses [?] · 1479 ratings · 435 pages · Published: 1813
When Elizabeth Bennet first meets eligible bachelor Fitzwilliam Darcy, she thinks him arrogant and conceited, while he struggles to remain indifferent to her good looks and lively mind. When she later discovers that Darcy has involved himself in the troubled relationship between his friend Bingley and her beloved sister Jane, she is determined to dislike him more than ever.

In the sparkling comedy of manners that follows, Jane Austen shows the folly of judging by first impressions and superbly evokes the friendships, gossip and snobberies of provincial middle-class life. Rated '1/5 - Glimpses and kisses' for steam/spice/heat level.

      I would give the novel P&P five stars and the annotations two. This is a sad excuse for an annotated edition. I learned very little that was new from the annotations -- many of them were so elementary even if you were unfamiliar with Austen and/or Regency England you would still find them unnecessary. The bigger problem with the annotations was that many of them were not annotations but rather Shapard's personal commentary on the characters or story. I strongly disagreed with many of this comments and fear they would color the opinion of new P&P readers instead of letting them form their own opinions. Basically, I would recommend this to someone like my husband: has never read P&P so would find the factual (but basic) annotations helpful, but has me at his side to correct Shapard's misleading commentary.

      I was first introduced into the world of Jane Austen by the 1995 BBC adaptation of this novel (as I am sure many others were). This has since become one of my favourite books to return to time and time again. While not my favourite Jane Austen novel, it certainly holds a special place in my heart and I have actually lost track of how many times I have actually re-read this book.

      It admittedly is not an easy read. It was written over 200 years ago, and the language shows. If one can overlook the old fashion ideals and leave the current day behind while you read it, it is a very enjoyable book. In a way, you almost need to suspend your disbelief and enter into as if it were another world entirely, just as one would with a sci-fi or fantasy novel. It's just a different kind of escape.

      I recently found a great video on YouTube that really helps with the understanding of the novel (and, to a greater extent, the rest of Jane Austen's novels). Even I feel like I benefitted from having watched it and listened to what the poster had to say. Ten Things to Know before Reading "Pride and Prejudice"

    • fearless-leader rated it
      6 months ago
      Yes, I am a literary buff who's read basically the entire Western canon but I've been avoiding this book for years, mainly due to the cult of Darcy. I can't think of a more misguided fandom. This isn't a romance at all, it's a comedy of manners, an anti-romance about not trusting those romance feelings. If anything it's very mercenary, per the values of the time--Darcy turns out to be loveable, but it's in no small part because he's very wealthy and able to use his money and influence to help the Bennets. I found it difficult to put down, and genuinely hilarious in places. Mr. Collins, especially his inability to understand a refusal, was both funny and painfully recognizable 200 years later.

      Overall, so much more literate/socially aware than I was led to believe. Makes me want to go on an Austen kick. Definitely not about sexy brooding gentlemen.
